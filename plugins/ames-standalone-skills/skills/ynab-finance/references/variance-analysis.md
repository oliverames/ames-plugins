# Variance Analysis

Techniques for understanding why spending differed from budget, identifying patterns, and making informed budget adjustments.

## Types of Comparisons

### Budget vs Actual

The primary comparison. For each YNAB category, compare what you budgeted to what you actually spent.

- **Over budget**: Spent more than planned. Is it a one-time spike or a pattern?
- **Under budget**: Spent less than planned. Genuine savings or deferred spending?
- **On budget**: Within a reasonable range (within 10-15% is typical for variable categories)

### Month-over-Month

Compare this month's spending to last month's. Useful for spotting trends:
- Gradually increasing spending in a category
- Seasonal patterns (heating in winter, activities in summer)
- New recurring expenses that crept in

### Rolling Average

Compare to your 3-month or 6-month average. More stable than month-over-month, smooths out one-time spikes.

### Year-over-Year

Compare to the same month last year. Best for categories with strong seasonal patterns.

## Breaking Down Variances

When spending is significantly different from budget, break it down:

### By Transaction Size

- **A few large transactions**: Easy to identify and explain (annual insurance premium, car repair, medical bill)
- **Many small transactions**: Harder to spot — look for frequency increases (more dining out, more Amazon orders)

### By Timing

- **Early/late bills**: A bill that usually hits on the 1st might land on the last day of the prior month
- **Annual or quarterly charges**: Insurance, subscriptions, property taxes that don't hit every month
- **Reimbursable expenses**: Money spent now but reimbursed later

### By Category Accuracy

Sometimes the variance is really a categorization issue:
- Groceries vs dining out (was that Costco trip food or household supplies?)
- Shopping vs gifts
- Home maintenance vs home improvement

## Spending Trend Analysis

Track category spending over time to spot patterns:

```
Monthly Spending: Groceries
  Oct:    $850
  Nov:    $920   (+8%)
  Dec:    $1,100 (+20%) -- holiday cooking
  Jan:    $880   (-20%) -- back to normal
  Budget: $900
```

What to look for:
- **Creeping increases**: Spending rising 5-10% each month without a clear reason
- **Seasonal spikes**: Predictable peaks you can plan for next year
- **Step changes**: A permanent increase (new family member, price increases, lifestyle change)

## When to Adjust the Budget

Adjust the YNAB budget when:
- You're consistently over budget in a category for 3+ months (budget is unrealistic)
- You're consistently under budget (money could be allocated elsewhere)
- A life change has permanently altered spending (new car payment, kid in school)
- Seasonal patterns are predictable enough to budget for

Don't adjust for:
- One-time spikes (car repair, medical bill) — use YNAB's "stuff I forgot to budget for" approach
- A single bad month — wait to see if it's a pattern
- Small variances under 10-15% in variable categories — that's normal

## Savings Rate Analysis

Track monthly and as a rolling average. Common benchmarks:
- 10-15%: Solid baseline for retirement savings
- 20%+: Accelerated savings or debt payoff
- 50%+: Aggressive financial independence path

If your savings rate is declining, the variance analysis will show which categories are driving the change.
