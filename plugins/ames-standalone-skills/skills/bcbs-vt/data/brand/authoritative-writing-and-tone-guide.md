## Writing Goals (every communication aims to be)

- **Inspiring.** Speak from an appropriately positive tone — genuine, not untruthful. Even about difficult things (health conditions, denials, regulatory matters), give audiences positive courses of action.
- **Friendly and polite.** Communicate as Vermonters serving Vermonters, not as an insurance company talking to members. Picture a friend.
- **Clear and direct.** Sixth-grade reading level or lower. Have a colleague review and confirm they understood.
- **Empathetic.** Ask yourself: If I were receiving this, how would I feel, what would I want to know, and how would I want to be spoken to?
- **Truthful.** Operate from integrity; don't conceal valuable facts or mislead.
- **Uniquely Vermont.** We're Vermont's only local health plan, made up of Vermonters. No other plan can say that.

**Content should be:**

- **Clear.** Understand the topic. Simple words and sentences. Concise.
- **Useful.** Ask: What purpose? Who reads it? What do they need to know?
- **Supportive.** Write like a human. Break rules if it makes writing more accessible.
- **Appropriate.** Match tone to situation, just like face-to-face.

## Voice vs. Tone

Voice doesn't change much from day to day. Tone changes all the time — one tone when comforting someone scared, another when celebrating with someone laughing.

**Blue Cross VT voice:**
- **Plainspoken.** Navigating health care is confusing, intimidating, overwhelming — we make it easier.
- **Genuine.** We relate to customers' challenges and speak to them in a familiar, warm, accessible way.
- **Translators.** Only experts can make what's difficult look easy.

**Tone:** Friendly, but always more important to be clear than entertaining. Consider the reader's state of mind.

## Core Style Tips

- **Active voice.** "Marti logged into the account" — not "The account was logged into by Marti." Exception: when the subject is irrelevant and you want to emphasize the action — "Your account was flagged by our Abuse team."
- **Avoid slang and jargon.** Plain English. If a technical term is needed, briefly define it.
- **Write positively.** "To get a cookie, stand in line" — not "You can't get a cookie if you don't stand in line."
- **Format to guide the reader.** Subheads, bullets, paragraph breaks.

## Writing About Ourselves

Company name: **Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Vermont.** We also manage two affiliates: **Vermont Blue Advantage** and **The Vermont Health Plan.**

- When referring to ourselves, do **not** abbreviate to "BCBSVT."
- Use **"Blue Cross"** and **"we," "our."**
- In more formal communications or communications that may include another Blue plan or cross state lines, **"Blue Cross VT"** can be used.
- First introduction: "Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Vermont (Blue Cross VT)." After that, "Blue Cross VT."
- Internal messages: "Blue Cross," "our company," or "our organization."

## Writing About People

Audiences are made up of real people. Always use **"they"** instead of "it." Use **"who"** (not "that") when referring to a person: *Jake is an engaged member who called to learn more.*

### People-First Language

Only refer to someone's abilities, medical conditions, or mental health if it's relevant. Recognize the person as an individual, not defined by their condition.

- ✅ "Mary has bipolar disorder." "Sam is addicted to substances."
- ❌ "Mary is bipolar." "Sam is an addict."

Never assume someone has a medical, mental, or cognitive condition. Avoid "courageous" or "inspirational" when referring to someone with a disability or medical condition doing everyday activities (e.g., saying someone who has cancer is brave for going to work).

### Inclusive Language Lists

**Abilities, conditions, mental health:**
- ✅ people with disabilities, people experiencing mental illness, people who have [condition], people living with [condition], in recovery, children with special needs, differently abled, individuals with disabilities, disabled
- ❌ Mary is bipolar, lame, addict, alcoholic, diabetic, victim, crazy/insane/nuts/psycho, dumb, handicapped *(except "handicapped parking," which is OK)*

**Age:**
- ✅ aging, experienced, mature, older, seasoned, older adults, seniors, youth, young adults, older people, older individuals
- ❌ elderly, grandma/grandpa, geriatric, girl/boy, old, of a certain age, over the hill, senior citizen, young (as in "80-years-young," "young lady")

**Gender and sexuality:**
- Avoid gendered terms: use "server" instead of "waitress," "businessperson" instead of "businessman"
- Don't use gender when talking about profession ("female CEO," "male nurse")
- ✅ everyone, all, folks, team, chairperson, humankind, police officer
- ❌ chairman, females, males, gals/girls, guys, ladies, mankind, man hours, policeman
- Use "same-sex" marriage only if the distinction is relevant. Avoid "gay marriage." It's all "marriage."
- ✅ lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender *(never "transgendered")*, trans, queer/questioning, LGBTQ, LGBTQ+, cisgender, gender fluid, non-binary
- ❌ homosexual, lifestyle, preference, transvestite, describing something as "gay"

**Pronouns:** Embrace "they" as a singular pronoun. Inclusive: he/his, she/hers, they/them, nonbinary, ze/zir. Use the person's communicated pronouns; if unknown, ask or use their name. Avoid Mr./Mrs./Ms.

**Hearing:** ✅ "deaf," "partially deaf," "hard of hearing," "people with hearing loss." ❌ "hearing impaired."

**Heritage and nationality:** Only reference if relevant. Don't use hyphens in dual heritage ("Asian American," not "Asian-American"). Capitalize Black in racial/ethnic/cultural sense. Capitalize Indigenous when referring to original inhabitants. Avoid "grandfather" (as in "grandfather clause" — rooted in voter suppression); use "legacy," "old rule," "inherited," or "precedent." "Tribe" and "reservation" are used in official federal names but should not be used to reference groups ("my tribe"); similarly avoid "powwow" for a business meeting.
- ✅ Asian American/Pacific Islander, Black/African American, Latino/a or Hispanic, Native American, American Indian/Alaskan Native, White, Indigenous, LatinX, people of color, tribal communities, biracial, multiracial
- ❌ Caucasian, Brown, master/slave, mixed-race, non-white, peanut gallery, tribe, reservation, powwow

**Socio-Economic:**
- ✅ people experiencing homelessness, people with limited economic security, low-income, low-income neighborhood, under-resourced, people facing homelessness, neighborhoods with high poverty rates, people in need, food insecure
- ❌ barrio, ghetto, inner city, homeless people, needy, poor, thug, urban

**Spirituality/religion/non-affiliation:** Only reference if relevant. Use date references or "winter holidays" rather than "Christmas"; same for "Easter" ("spring," "spring holidays," or the date).
- ✅ religious communities, spiritual, clergy, imam, rabbi, religious leader, pastor, person of the cloth, reverend, dietary restrictions such as keeping kosher or fasting, agnostic or unaffiliated people, atheist, person of faith, place of worship
- ❌ faith-less, moral-less, "church" as a catchall for house of worship, terms like "kosher" when not referring to a specific dietary code

**Vision:** ✅ "blind," "low vision," "people with low vision," "people who are blind." ❌ "the blind."

## Grammar and Mechanics

### Basics

- Write for all readers (some skim, some read every word)
- Focus your message — lead with the most important content
- Be concise, specific, consistent

### Text Formatting

- **Don't use underline formatting** or any combination of italic, bold, caps, and underline
- **Left-align text**, never center or right-aligned
- **One space between sentences**, never two
- **Font:** Calibri, DIN 2014, or Arial
- **Size:** 11 pt, or 12 pt for Medicare-related communications

### Abbreviations and Acronyms

If the reader may not recognize it, spell it out first with the abbreviation in parentheses. Then use the short form.

- First use: Consumer and Business Support (CBSS). Second use: CBSS.
- First use: Vermont Health Connect (VHC). Second use: VHC.

Well-known abbreviations (VT, HTML) can be used without spelling out.

### Capitalization

We use **title case** in communications, including PowerPoints, letters, articles, and press releases. Title case capitalizes every word except articles, prepositions, and conjunctions. **We do not use sentence case.**

Email addresses and URLs: all lowercase. Examples: *communications@bcbsvt.com, bcbsvt.com, vermontblueadvantage.com*.

Never capitalize: website · internet · online · email. Capitalize **Web** when referring to the world wide web.

Capitalize individual job titles when referencing a specific role ("Our new Marketing Manager starts today"), but not in general terms ("All the managers ate fruit"). Don't refer to someone as a "ninja," "rockstar," or "wizard" unless they literally are one.

Capitalize department/team names but not "team" or "department" itself: *Marketing team, Support department.*

### Contractions

Great — use them freely for an informal, friendly tone.

### Emoji

Infrequent and deliberate. Social media or internal friendly email only. **Never use emoji in formal correspondence.**

### Italics

Use for long-work titles (books, movies, albums) and for emphasis. Also for digital element citations and navigation labels in step-by-step instructions:
- When you're all done, click *Send.*
- A/B testing variables — *Subject line, From name,* and *Send time.*

### Numbers

- Spell out whole numbers below 10 (and any number beginning a sentence). Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Use numerals for height, percentages, placement, etc. — even for numbers under 10.
- Numbers over 3 digits get commas: 999 / 1,000.
- Write big numbers out; abbreviate only for space: 1k, 150k.

**Dates:** Spell out day and month: *Saturday, January 24.* Abbreviate only if space demands: *Sat., Jan. 24.*

**Decimals/fractions:** Spell out fractions (two-thirds, not 2/3). Use decimals when not easily expressed as a fraction (1.375).

**Money:** US currency uses `$` before the amount. For international contexts, use 3-letter abbreviations (USD, CAD): "25 USD, not $25." Avoid colloquial ("five-and-dime," "greenbacks").

**Percentages:** Spell out "percent" unless pressed for space. Use numerals in all percent figures.

**Ranges:** Hyphen, no spaces: "It takes 20-30 days."

**Telephone numbers:** Parentheses then dash without spaces: (555) 867-5309. Country code for international readers: +1-404-123-4567.

**Temperature:** Use degree symbol with capital F for Fahrenheit.

**Time:** Use numerals and "a.m." / "p.m." with a space. Don't use minutes on the hour: *7 a.m.*, *7:30 p.m.* Hyphen between times for ranges: *7 a.m.–10:30 p.m.*

**Time zones:** Specify when scheduling. Default EST (Vermont). Abbreviate continental US: EST, CST, MST, PST. International: spell out (Nepal Standard Time).

**Decades:** Within the past 100 years, no apostrophe: *the 00s, the 90s.* More than 100 years ago, be specific: *the 1900s, the 1890s.*

### Punctuation

- **Ampersands:** Avoid unless part of a company name (*Ben & Jerry's*).
- **Apostrophes:** Possessive rules — add 's to singular nouns (even ending in s: *Chris's cookie*); just ' to plural nouns ending in s (*managers' cookies*); 's to plural nouns not ending in s (*women's*).
- **Colons:** Use for lists (not ellipsis, em dash, or comma). Can join two related phrases; capitalize after colon if a complete sentence follows.
- **Commas — use the serial (Oxford) comma.**
- **Dashes and hyphens:** Hyphen (-) for single-phrase links, ranges/spans (no spaces). Em dash (—) — no spaces — for asides. **Use a true em dash, not "-" or "--".**
- **Ellipses:** Inside brackets `[...]` to indicate omitted quote words.
- **Exclamation points:** Sparingly. Never more than one. Never in failure/alert messages. Go inside quotation marks; outside parentheses when parenthetical is part of a larger sentence.
- **Periods:** Inside quotation marks. Single space between sentences.
- **Question marks:** Inside quotation marks if part of the quote.
- **Quotation marks:** Periods and commas inside. Single quotes for quotes within quotes.
- **Semicolons:** Go easy — they usually support long, complicated sentences that could be simplified. Prefer an em dash or new sentence.

### Names, Titles, Schools, Quotes

- First mention: full first and last name. Subsequent: last name only.
- Capitalize individual job titles when referencing the role, but not in general terms.
- Present tense for quotes: *"We're Vermonters serving Vermonters," says Jamie Smith.*
- First mention of a school: full official name, then common abbreviation.

### States, Cities, and URLs

- Spell out all city and state names. Don't abbreviate city names.
- Spell out "United States" as a noun. Use "U.S." only as an adjective.
- **If mentioning a Vermont county or town, you do not need to add "Vermont" or "VT."**
- Capitalize website names; don't italicize.
- Avoid spelling out URLs; when you must, drop the `http://www.` prefix.

### File Extensions

General type: uppercase without period, add lowercase s for plural (*GIF, PDF, HTML, JPGs*). Specific filename: lowercase (*slowclap.gif*).

## Writing for Accessibility

As you write, ask:
- Would this language make sense to someone who doesn't work here?
- Could someone quickly scan this document and understand?
- If someone can't see the colors, images, or video, is the message still clear?
- Is the markup clean and structured?
- Does this work well on mobile devices with accessibility features?

### Guidelines

- **Avoid directional language.** ✅ "Select from these options" (steps listed after the title). ❌ "Select from the options in the right sidebar."
- **Be mindful of visual elements.** High contrast. Images shouldn't be the only method of communication.
- **Beware multi-meaning words** like "once" (could mean one time / after / in the past / when), "right" (correct / opposite of left / politically conservative), "since" (point in time / because), "require plus an infinitive," "has/have plus past participle."
- **Employ a hierarchy.** Most important information first. True lists, not paragraphs with line breaks.
- **Label forms** with clear names and appropriate tags. Think carefully about which fields are necessary, which are required. Shorter is better.
- **Make sure closed captioning is available** for all videos; supplement with transcripts where possible.
- **Use alt text** on all images.
- **Use descriptive links.** Avoid "click here" or "learn more."
- **Use headers** nested and consecutive — never skip levels for styling.
- **Use plain language.** Short sentences, familiar words. Explain abbreviations/acronyms.

## Letter Guidelines

A good letter has a beginning, middle, and ending.
- **Beginning:** introduce who we are and what the letter is about; set the stage for later requests.
- **Middle:** the call to action — clear, with steps broken into bullets or headings. Remind the reader how they'll benefit.
- **Ending:** positive note. Invitation to call with questions. Personalized with your name.

### Letter Checklist

**Content:**
- [ ] I have shared why I'm writing the letter
- [ ] I did not use any industry jargon or overly complex terms
- [ ] I read the letter out loud to catch any strange phrasing or awkward terms
- [ ] I checked my punctuation and my spelling
- [ ] My call to action is in the first paragraph
- [ ] I've verified all phone numbers and URLs
- [ ] I've double checked all business hours and email addresses
- [ ] I have checked with the person signing the letter to make sure they know I am sending it
- [ ] I have checked for any sensitive data in the mailing — if it is not necessary, I have excluded it
- [ ] I have thanked the recipient
- [ ] There is an actual person's signature/contact information

**Format (verbatim from the Style Guide):**
- [ ] The font Calibri, Din 2014 or Arial
- [ ] The font point size 11
- [ ] The font point size 12 for Medicare-related communications
- [ ] The document is single spaced (1.0) in Microsoft Word
- [ ] The margins are set at: top=1", bottom=1", left=1.25", right=1.25"
- [ ] The correct service marks (e.g., ®, ℠) in logos, product names, etc. appear
- [ ] The logo is correct and appears on the first page — it is not stretched or blurry
- [ ] My letter includes the word "over" to tell the reader to turn the page if it is more than one page long
- [ ] My text is left-justified
- [ ] I have included a signature

## Copyright, Trademarks, Citations

**Copyright** protects original works fixed in a tangible medium (writings, recordings, drawings). Not names, facts, ideas, characters, or living things like gardens. Protection begins at creation; US enforcement requires registration with the US Copyright Office.

**Other creators' copyrights:** Obtain a license. A typical license spells out where/how long the work can be used, exclusivity, what we can do with it, restrictions (e.g., online but not billboard). Work-for-hire language commonly reads: *"You grant Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Vermont a perpetual, worldwide, non-exclusive, royalty free license to display, distribute, and publish the Work in our marketing in any medium now known or later developed."* Contact the Legal team.

**Image use:** Get permission first; credit the creator and link to the source. Google Image Search results are **not** licensed for fair use. Stock photo sites and Flickr Creative Commons are options.

**Registering trademarks:** Work with BCBSA and the Legal team. File a derivative request if "Blue" appears in the name. Reach out to Brand and Engagement Strategies and Legal for new products.

**Citations:** Copying language or images directly from the internet is plagiarism — a serious offense. Cite original sources; link out where possible.

**Exception — citing the US Government:** US government works are generally not subject to copyright. Anyone may reproduce, create derivatives, perform publicly, display, or distribute without restriction under US copyright laws (unless under a specific exception).
