### Enhanced Cost Estimate Tool Now Available
*Category: Understanding Insurance*

> You can get cost estimates for repairs to your vehicle or house. Why should health care be any different? Blue Cross VT members can find out in advance how much a health care procedure will cost, using the enhanced cost estimate tool now available in the Member Resource Center.
>
> With our cost estimate tool, you can see how much your out-of-pocket cost will be for many types of health care, such as office visits, diagnostic imaging, and procedures like knee replacement. This can help you plan for upcoming health care.
>
> You can also use the tool to compare prices between different health care providers and facilities. The cost of a procedure like magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) can vary greatly, depending on where it is done. Larger hospitals can charge $6,000 or more for an MRI, while the cost for the same procedure at independent facility may be less than $2,000. Our cost estimate tool helps you identify which locations cost less, potentially saving you hundreds or even thousands of dollars if you haven't met your annual deductible.
>
> Please keep in mind that the figures you'll see are only estimates. The actual costs may vary depending on several factors, including how the provider bills us for the procedure.
>
> Our new cost estimate tool greatly enhances your power as a health care consumer to shop around and make informed choices. When planning for health care, we hope you'll use our cost estimate tool to compare options and save money.
>
> Giving our members immediate access to cost estimates is part of our mission to make health care work better for Vermonters.

### The Impact of Stress on Heart Health: Andrew's Story
*By: [unnamed] | Feb 12, 2026 | Category: Health & Wellness*

> On a warm fall Saturday, Blue Cross VT employee Andrew Garland was working outside in his yard. He'd recently had two large trees taken down, and he and an acquaintance were chopping wood and carrying it across the lawn. It was heavy physical labor, but not out of the ordinary.
>
> About a half hour into working, Andrew became uncharacteristically exhausted and short of breath. He took breaks, but he couldn't recover his energy. He loaded more wood into a wheelbarrow and attempted to push it across the lawn, but he only made it halfway before doubling over.
>
> Inside the house, Andrew drank electrolytes to no result. An hour after drinking a sports drink, Andrew was still exhausted and breathing heavily.
>
> Barely walking, Andrew took a shower and decided he had to go to urgent care. He recalled the story of a Blue Cross VT coworker who'd had a heart attack a few years earlier. But Andrew didn't have any of the usual symptoms like chest pain or sensation in his left arm. He just felt exhausted and strange.
>
> Before departing, Andrew opened a web browser and searched: "Can you have a heart attack without chest pain?"

**[Section: A Silent Heart Attack]**
> On his way to urgent care, Andrew's intuition rerouted him to the emergency room. Upon arrival, it appeared that Andrew was having a panic attack, which can have similar symptoms of a heart attack — shortness of breath, dizziness, and digestive distress. However, blood work revealed that troponin, a protein that exists in heart muscle cells, was present at very high levels. This indicated heart damage.
>
> Doctors confirmed that Andrew was having a heart attack.
>
> Andrew stayed in the hospital for four days, during which he was thoroughly examined. After an EKG, MRI, and heart catheterization, an imaging procedure where a tiny tube is threaded into the heart, doctors found no blockages or structural problems that are commonly present when people experience heart attacks.
>
> Doctors determined that stress was a contributing factor to Andrew's condition. "Stress triggers the release of hormones that can increase blood pressure and heart rate, narrow blood vessels, and cause inflammation and plaque build-up." Another contributing factor was undiagnosed celiac disease.

**[Section: Managing Stress for Heart Health]**
> A few months later, Andrew is doing much better. He no longer eats wheat or other gluten-containing foods, and he has made lifestyle changes to help manage stress. Some of the resources in Andrew's toolkit include:
>
> - **Meditation and conscious breathing:** Just four or five slow, mindful breaths can help relax tension in the mind and body. Andrew likes to think "in" on the inhale and "out" on the exhale to help focus his mind.
> - **Progressive muscle relaxation:** Alternately contracting and relaxing your muscles, focusing your attention on one region of the body at a time, can have a calming effect. Starting with his feet, Andrew engages his muscles on an inhalation, holds for a few seconds, and then relaxes with an exhalation.
> - **Boundaries:** Something Andrew learned from a coworker is to keep an imaginary box in the corner of his office, and if something is nagging him at the end of the workday, he can put it in the box and know it'll be there when he returns.
> - **Community:** The health benefits of connecting with community are innumerable. Andrew is making an effort to get out and be around other people, even if they're strangers at a concert or restaurant.
> - **Therapy:** Mental health counselors can provide support during major life changes such as illness and injury.
> - **Limiting the news:** One of the biggest impacts we can have on our health is to tend to our personal stress rather than worrying about what's beyond our control.
> - **Doing what you love:** Since Andrew's celiac diagnosis, he's been more dedicated to cooking at home, which he loves to do.
>
> In honor of Heart Health Month, consider trying one thing from this list today.

### Forming Healthy Habits Using Be Well Vermont
*Jan 23, 2026 | Category: Understanding Insurance*

> At the start of the year, many people try to adopt healthy habits like eating balanced meals or drinking more water. However, as the weeks go by, it becomes harder to maintain those practices. The Be Well Vermont platform can help you form healthy habits that last.
>
> The reason people often fail to form healthy habits is they attempt large behavior changes, says Kim L., a Blue Cross VT health and wellness consultant. This approach usually leads to burnout and a return to previous patterns.
>
> A more successful approach, Kim says, is to make tiny habit changes. "It's small nudges over time that can make the difference long term," she explains. Be Well Vermont uses this method, which is based on the work of B.J. Fogg, PhD, a behavior scientist at Stanford University who authored the bestselling book, Tiny Habits.
>
> For example, instead of trying to adopt the habit of drinking 64 ounces of water a day, a Be Well Vermont healthy habit is simply drinking a glass of water after waking up. "It's taking bigger behaviors that you're hoping to get to, where you are drinking the recommended amount of water, and taking baby steps toward it that are really achievable over time," Kim says.

## VT Affordable Care Campaign (vtaffordablecare.com)
*Launched Dec 2025 | Coalition: Blue Cross VT + Northwestern Medical Center + Green Mountain Surgery Center + Vermont OPEN Imaging + Vermont Diagnostic Imaging*

**Campaign tagline:**
> "Each of us can help lower the cost of our health care without sacrificing quality."

**Main headline:**
> "Vermonters Pay the Most in America for Healthcare"

**Key statistic:**
> "Vermonters now spend 19.6% of their income on healthcare, compared to the national average of just 7.9%. That's the highest in the country."

Source: U.S. Census Bureau and Kaiser Family Foundation data, WalletHub July 2025 report.

**Consumer empowerment frame:**
> "There are practical steps each of us can take to reduce expenses without sacrificing quality of care."

**Price comparison example:**
MRI at UVM Medical Center: $6,520 | Northwestern Medical Center: $2,785 | Independent facility average: $1,799

**Blue Cross VT self-description in campaign materials:**
> "Vermont's only local, non-profit health plan for more than 40 years."

**Teresa Anderson, Director of Brand and Engagement Strategies, Blue Cross VT:**
> "We really wanted to educate Vermonters on how the cost of delivery of care impacts affordability, so that people could feel empowered to make informed decisions."
