## 2026-05-07 Premium-Setting Blog Social Caption (Teams chat review)

Cross-channel social caption drafted to amplify the approved blog "How is the Cost of Your Health Insurance Premium Decided?" — reviewed across Teams chat by Ashley Legacy (Brand Lead), Kristina Massari, and Gina Brittain.

**Brand Lead (Ashley Legacy):** "I think we need to note that what we do is for the rate filing... we don't do it each month. Just thinking of the flow from the first to second sentences."
*Pattern: Cadence implication in social copy. When a sentence uses a member-experienced cadence ("your *monthly* health care premium") and the next sentence immediately describes operational work (analyzing claims, forecasting costs, factoring in regulatory changes), readers can infer the operational work happens at the member cadence. In reality, rate-setting is annual — the rate filing happens once a year. Fix: either reset the cadence in the next sentence ("Each year, Blue Cross VT files our rates with the Green Mountain Care Board...") or avoid the monthly framing in the opener. This is a logical-flow catch that depends on operator knowledge of the actual rate-filing rhythm; rules-based checks (department names, Medicare compliance, em-dash use) will not surface it.*

**Reviewer (Kristina Massari, suggesting an adjective):** "It's a meticulous process that starts with reviewing last year's claims..."
*Pattern: Promotional-adjective creep. Reviewers may suggest adjectives that "strengthen" copy by labeling our process favorably ("meticulous," "thorough," "careful," "robust," "comprehensive"). For organic social, this tips the post from value-add toward self-promotion, which is the opposite of what organic copy should do. The structure of the sentences that follow (e.g., a factor list) typically does the work of showing rigor without us telling readers our process is rigorous. Decline these suggestions and let the next sentence carry the weight. Anchor pushback in the principle that organic social must read as value-add for the reader, not promotion of us.*

**Reviewer (Kristina Massari, suggesting a structural move):** Moved "Each year, Blue Cross VT files our rates with the Green Mountain Care Board" from paragraph 2 to paragraph 4 in a redraft.
*Pattern: Structural moves can unintentionally undo prior cadence fixes. When a member-experienced cadence term ("monthly premium") in the opener is followed by an operational description, the cadence reset must come BEFORE the description, not at the end of the post. Confirm any reviewer-suggested structural moves don't reintroduce a cadence implication another reviewer flagged earlier in the same thread. Pattern often appears when a second reviewer who hasn't read the prior round suggests a "more logical" reordering.*

**Reviewer (Gina Brittain, raising regulatory-optics concern):** "Given our very public history with our regulators -- and the optics for the additional hands-on oversight they required of our organization amid our financial issues -- I feel strongly about including the fact that all major insurance plans in VT must file with the GMCB at the top of the post."
*Pattern: Regulatory-positioning defense. When BCBS-specific copy describes regulatory processes (rate filings, hearings, oversight, agreements), it can inadvertently reinforce a prior regulatory narrative readers may remember. If BCBS has had recent or ongoing public regulatory action, lead body content with the universe of regulated entities ("all major insurance plans in Vermont, including Blue Cross VT, must file...") rather than naming BCBS in isolation. This elevates from preference to closer-to-red-flag when an operator-aware reviewer signals current legislative-optics context (per Ashley Legacy's confirmation in the same thread: "the legislative optics... are eh right now"). Surgical fix is a single-sentence body-leading change, not a full restructure.*

**Reviewer (Gina Brittain, suggesting "94 cents" reframe):** "94 cents of every premium dollar pays for care our members need."
*Pattern: Source-bounded precision. When reviewers suggest reframing for emotional emphasis ("flip the script," "lead with the positive"), check whether the new framing introduces a precision claim the source (the approved blog, press release, fact sheet) doesn't make. Numerical inversions are especially prone to this: "less than 6 cents to admin" does NOT equal "94 cents to care," because the remaining 94 cents may split among care, reserves, taxes/fees, and other categories the blog also lists. If the spirit of the reframe is good, honor it with language that matches the source's level of precision ("most of every premium dollar pays for the care our members rely on") rather than importing an unbacked specific figure.*

**Resolution pattern:** Oliver held the line on the "meticulous" adjective and the structural move, conceded the regulatory-positioning point after Ashley confirmed the legislative-optics context, and pushed back on the 94-cent reframe by offering vaguer source-aligned language. The full thread illustrates the red-flag-vs-preference triage skill — the writer's job is to engage on substance, take what's actually a red flag (regulatory positioning, in this case), and decline what's preference dressed as a directive.

**Brand Lead (Ashley Legacy, on share-back protocol mid-thread):** "I think we can just take her final note and then leave it to just us....the more we share back, the more it will continue to be edited."
*Pattern: Review-cycle share-back limits. Each draft share-back to peers and stakeholders invites another round of edits, even from friendly reviewers. Once a reviewer signals satisfaction with a specific change, stop sharing the post back. Internal-team revisions stay internal. The pattern compounds because friendly reviewers continue finding small things to refine if they keep seeing drafts. Reduce visibility surface to reduce editing opportunity. Most applicable when the post is approved by the manager, the reviewer is a peer (not in the formal approval chain), and the remaining edits are clarity or preference rather than red flags. Pairs with the red-flag-vs-preference triage pattern: triage handles WHAT to take; share-back protocol handles WHO keeps seeing iterations.*

**Brand Lead (Ashley Legacy, on non-social-reader register, multiple comments):**

- "What is this?" (on "SHIP")
- "We don't use X so should be removed from reference." (on "X")

*Pattern: Acronym and abbreviation expansion. Any acronym or social-team shorthand must be defined on first use in any doc read by non-team audiences. Common offenders: SLA, SHIP, CS, "demo," platform initialisms. Define or replace at first use.*

**Brand Lead (Ashley Legacy, on cadence realism, multiple comments):**

Preferred framing: "build toward X by Q3, starting at Y" or "target mix is X, Y, Z; not every week will hit all three." **Brand Lead (Ashley Legacy, on borrowed benchmarks):**

Without staging, reviewers read brand-to-brand banter, new-platform-as-primary framings, or controversial topics as active plans and start asking resourcing questions. Staging framings: "consider for later phases," "active evaluation in late 2026," "a future-state opportunity once X matures."*

*Pattern: Negative-framing detection. Strategy and member-facing copy reads stronger when it describes what we will do, not what we won't. Flag "X beats Y" or "we don't do Z" used as the lead.*
