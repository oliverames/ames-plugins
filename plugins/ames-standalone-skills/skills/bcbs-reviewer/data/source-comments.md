# Source Comment Corpus

Sanitized review comments extracted from 10 BCBS VT documents (7 retrieved 2026-05-01, 3 additional). This file is the evidence base for the reviewer personas and checklist in this skill. Names have been replaced with role labels.

Included: comments that encode a review pattern (recurring feedback, editorial corrections, regulatory flags, audience calls).
Excluded: conversational filler, cost-center data, internal IT/hiring discussions, and one-off operational context that is not a review pattern.

Resolved comments are included — they document the intended correction and are often more useful than the unresolved flag alone.

## 2026-04-27-Google-Reviews-Response-Plan.xlsx

All comments below are from the Brand Lead, reviewing response copy templates on the "Reviews & Responses" sheet.

**On a response that promised follow-up:** "I don't know that we can promise our team will look in to issues like this; I also don't know that we want to share a specific dept (we don't have member services as a dept, assume you meant CS). I think we can say we appreciate the feedback and will share with the appropriate team.... I don't want them to expect a follow-up as issues like this could take months for prioritization."
*Pattern: Don't promise follow-up or name specific departments. Use "appropriate team." Don't set resolution-timeline expectations. "Member services" is not a BCBS VT department — use "Customer Service."*

**On a response that needed a direct contact:** "On this one, I think we want to offer a specific outreach - maybe our communications inbox?"
*Pattern: For some reviews, a direct contact point (communications inbox) is more useful than a generic acknowledgment.*

**On a response that promised resolution:** "We have to be careful not to promise resolution as often times there is automation to processes and letters that happen beyond our control."
*Pattern: Many BCBS processes are automated — don't promise resolution in public responses.*

**On a review mentioning bank information:** "I don't know that we ever ask for bank info...this sounds suspicious to me for responding to....thoughts?"
*Pattern: Flag suspicious reviews referencing things BCBS does not do — consult internally before responding.*

**On cost-related response language:** "Using words like heavy may not align well with our affordability messaging. I would remove that 'we hear how...' sentence. Before we promise what CS (not member services ;-)) can do, we need to confirm with the CS team first. I don't want us to overpromise solutions."
*Pattern: Avoid "heavy" in cost/coverage contexts — conflicts with affordability messaging. Confirm CS capacity before making commitments. Don't overpromise.*

**On an overly casual response draft:** "For brand voice we haven't typically used phrases like 'a lot of moving parts' or 'landed well'. I understand wanting to be approachable but this may sway a little too casual. =)"
*Pattern: Brand voice in responses — warm and approachable, but not overly casual. Flagged phrases: "a lot of moving parts," "landed well."*

**On a response addressing wait times:** "While we want to acknowledge the challenge, I also think we need to balance blame on our call center teams...we don't want to say times are long, etc. We know we have call wait times, but unfortunately don't need external facing to know resourcing behind the scene. In these types of reviews, I suggest sharing with the CS team first, seeing if they can assist and replying with a simple thank you we hear you and have shared with our team who should be able to assist."
*Pattern: Don't acknowledge internal operations (wait times, resourcing) publicly. Share with CS first; reply with simple acknowledgment.*

**On a low-rating review with no text:** "Similar to above with no comments, let's give a more direct contact option. There may be a desire for a little softer tone such as 'we would like to better understand your rating...'"
*Pattern: Low-rating, no-comment reviews: softer tone + direct contact option. Suggested phrasing: "we would like to better understand your rating."*

**On a sheet of testimonial quotes (separate sheet):** "You may be aware - at this time we can't use these in our materials/digital channels. It is a good tab to keep for future should we be able to but requires approval by Legal."
*Pattern: Google review testimonials require Legal approval before use in any marketing or digital channel — track them but do not publish without clearance.*

## 2026-04-30 Ashley & Oliver 1-on-1 — Transcript

Patterns derived from the verbal review discussion of the Google Reviews response drafts during the April 30, 2026 one-on-one. These expand on the cell comments already captured above; they came out of the back-and-forth conversation rather than written annotations.

**On responding to positive reviews that name specific employees:** "I honestly don't know if those people work here anymore from our perspective. So as you respond, it may just be like, 'thanks, this means a lot, we all care' — more generic, only because [Customer Service has] turnover."
*Pattern: Positive review responses that name specific BCBS employees should be kept generic. CS team turnover means named staff may no longer be with the organization. Use team-wide acknowledgment language ("we all care," "we'll make sure your kind words reach the right people") rather than calling out individuals.*

**On engaging with third-party posts where BCBS is mentioned:** "That was bad. They of course pulled out the one entity where they asked her basically about executive compensation and what she made. Did not go well, the optics of it... They posted on Instagram, all this stuff under Vermont Public's name. It wasn't pretty. We could watch it, but we're like, hey, this isn't our post."
*Pattern: A Blue Cross mention in a third-party post does not automatically warrant engagement. When a thread would surface sensitive topics (executive compensation, rate complaints, political positioning) or is already gaining negative momentum, watch-only is the right call. The bar for engagement is whether BCBS can add value — not simply that the brand was mentioned.*

**On political threads in paid social:** "Back in November, we ran through Crosby an ad tied to the VTaffordablecare.com campaign... it got political... people were working it out with each other... I was like, let's not engage in this conversation... It became multiple per day, long thread, a lot of banter, turned into political. I was like, I think we need to just watch now."
*Pattern: When paid ad comment threads turn political, the right call is watch-only. Don't engage even to correct misstatements — the thread momentum is more damaging than any clarification is useful. Teresa pulled the Crosby/VTaffordablecare.com ad after this experience.*

**On when to escalate response decisions:** "When a post straddles the line on engagement, bring to Ashley and Teresa. Most of the time I'll be able to quick draft a response and get it out the door to them. But if someone's saying you're failing the state and hard-working Vermonters while you pay your CEO a lot of money, that might be something to respond to, but there's no way I'm responding to that without getting Teresa's eyes on it."
*Pattern: When a public response decision is borderline — especially on executive-comp attacks, "you're failing Vermonters" framing, or any politically charged thread — bring the draft to the Brand Lead and Director before publishing. Most routine responses can be drafted and sent to them for a quick look; high-stakes responses require their eyes before anything goes out.*
